--------------------
Causality Condition
--------------------

For the relaxation-type theory considered here, causality requires

    (τ_J/(σ T)) (∂²P₀/∂α²) ≥ 1,

or, defining

    Λ ≡ τ_J/(σ T),

the condition becomes

    Λ (∂²P₀/∂α²) ≥ 1.

The equation of state is written as

    P(α,J²) = P₀(α) + (τ_J/(2σ)) J²,

where, for the present discussion, τ_J/σ is assumed to be independent of
α. The equilibrium contribution is

    P₀(T,α)
      = (95π²/180) T⁴
        + (T⁴/6) α²
        + (T⁴/108) α⁴.

Differentiating P₀ with respect to α while keeping T fixed gives

    ∂P₀/∂α
      = T⁴ (α/3 + α³/27),

and therefore

    ∂²P₀/∂α²
      = T⁴ (1/3 + α²/9).

The conductivity is taken to be

    σ = (15T)/(4π) [1/27 + α²/(243π²)].

Substituting these expressions into the causality condition yields

    (τ_J/(σT)) T⁴ (1/3 + α²/9) ≥ 1,

or equivalently,

    τ_J T³ (1/3 + α²/9) / σ ≥ 1.

Replacing σ by its explicit expression gives

    (4πτ_J T²/15)
    ×
    (1/3 + α²/9)
    -----------------------------------
    (1/27 + α²/(243π²))
    ≥ 1.

This inequality provides the minimum value of the relaxation time τ_J
required to preserve causality for a given temperature and chemical
potential parameter α.

Several observations follow immediately.

1.  The equilibrium susceptibility ∂²P₀/∂α² is always positive since

        ∂²P₀/∂α² = T⁴(1/3 + α²/9) > 0.

2.  The conductivity also remains positive,

        σ > 0,

    for every value of α.

3.  Consequently, the causality condition places a lower bound on the
    relaxation parameter τ_J. If τ_J is too small, signal propagation
    becomes acausal. Increasing τ_J restores causality by increasing the
    characteristic relaxation time of the dissipative current.

4.  Since both ∂²P₀/∂α² and σ depend on α, the required lower bound on
    τ_J also varies with the chemical potential. The competition between
    the increase of the thermodynamic susceptibility and the increase of
    the conductivity determines how restrictive the causality bound
    becomes as α increases.

This expression can be used directly to determine the admissible
parameter space for τ_J(T,α), or equivalently for the dimensionless
quantity Λ = τ_J/(σT), ensuring that the resulting hydrodynamic theory
remains causal.
