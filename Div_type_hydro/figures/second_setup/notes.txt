Comments: 
The dramatic improvement in training is a direct consequence of moving the system closer to equilibrium. By scaling both n and q down by 
10−2, we effectively reduced the amplitude of the perturbations around the background, 
which makes the PDE much easier to solve for both the PINN and the numerical method. Here is why.

Smaller initial amplitudes → smaller derivatives → more linear physics → balanced residuals → easier optimization → superior accuracy. 
The improvement we observed is the PINN telling us that it thrives in the regime where the underlying PDE is well‑behaved.

Training dynamics: the Adam log shows

• Loss drops steadily, with no sudden spikes.
• Both R1_rms and R2_rms decrease together, eventually reaching 10−4. 
• The learning rate decays smoothly. L‑BFGS starts from an already excellent state (1.7×10−7) and converges rapidly.

This is exactly what we expect when the optimization landscape is well‑conditioned: 
a convex‑like basin of attraction near the true solution.
