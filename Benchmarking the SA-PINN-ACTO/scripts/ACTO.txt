\paragraph{First-order periodic boundary conditions.}
For problems requiring continuity of both the solution and its spatial gradient across periodic boundaries, we enforce
\begin{equation}
    u(t,-L) = u(t,L), \qquad u_x(t,-L) = u_x(t,L).
\end{equation}
To embed these conditions algebraically, we introduce a correction polynomial that depends on the boundary mismatches of the raw network output $\tilde{u}_\theta(t,x)$. Define the zeroth- and first-order mismatches as
\begin{align}
    \Delta_0 &= \tilde{u}_\theta(t,L) - \tilde{u}_\theta(t,-L), \\
    \Delta_1 &= \partial_x \tilde{u}_\theta(t,L) - \partial_x \tilde{u}_\theta(t,-L).
\end{align}
We then construct the transformed field
\begin{equation}\label{eq:first_order_periodic}
    u_\theta(t,x)
    =
    \tilde{u}_\theta(t,x)
    +
    \frac{\Delta_0 - L\Delta_1}{4L^3} (x+L)^3
    +
    \frac{2L\Delta_1 - 3\Delta_0}{4L^2} (x+L)^2,
\end{equation}
where the correction term is a cubic polynomial in $x$ (with coefficients depending on $t$ through $\Delta_0$ and $\Delta_1$) designed to satisfy the four boundary constraints while preserving the interior structure of $\tilde{u}_\theta$.

To verify the enforcement, evaluate the transformation at the boundaries. At $x=-L$, the polynomial and its derivative vanish identically, giving
\[
    u_\theta(t,-L) = \tilde{u}_\theta(t,-L), \qquad
    u_{\theta,x}(t,-L) = \tilde{u}_{\theta,x}(t,-L).
\]
At $x=L$, direct substitution yields
\[
    u_\theta(t,L) = \tilde{u}_\theta(t,L) - \Delta_0 = \tilde{u}_\theta(t,-L),
\]
and for the derivative,
\[
    u_{\theta,x}(t,L) = \tilde{u}_{\theta,x}(t,L) - \Delta_1 = \tilde{u}_{\theta,x}(t,-L).
\]
Thus, both periodic conditions are satisfied exactly:
\[
    u_\theta(t,-L)=u_\theta(t,L), \qquad
    u_{\theta,x}(t,-L)=u_{\theta,x}(t,L).
\]

Consequently, the boundary loss term vanishes identically,
\[
    \mathcal{L}_{\rm BC}=0,
\]
and the total loss reduces to the PDE residual alone:
\[
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\rm PDE}.
\]
This transformation seamlessly extends the zeroth-order ACTO approach to first-order periodicities. Notably, it requires computing the spatial derivative of the raw network only at the boundary points, which is readily available via automatic differentiation. The resulting transformed network remains differentiable with respect to $t$ and $x$, as $\Delta_0$ and $\Delta_1$ depend on the boundary evaluations of $t$ (and network parameters) but are treated as constants with respect to the evaluation coordinate $x$ during the spatial derivative of the correction term. This ensures compatibility with standard gradient-based optimization for solving time-dependent PDEs.

Yes. Mathematically, the construction is sound. The polynomial correction you derived is exactly the type of lifting function used in finite element methods and constrained neural networks: you take an unconstrained network (\tilde u_\theta) and add a correction that cancels the boundary mismatches.

## Conceptually

This is exactly the natural extension of ACTO.

For zeroth-order periodicity,

[
u=\tilde u+\phi(x)\Delta_0,
]

where (\phi) is chosen so that

* (\phi(-L)=0),
* (\phi(L)=-1).

For first-order periodicity, you simply need one more degree of freedom. A cubic polynomial is the lowest-degree polynomial satisfying

[
P(-L)=0,\quad
P_x(-L)=0,\quad
P(L)=-\Delta_0,\quad
P_x(L)=-\Delta_1.
]

There are four constraints, so a cubic (four coefficients) is the minimal polynomial. Your choice with no constant or linear terms is particularly elegant because it automatically satisfies the left boundary conditions.

## One thing I would slightly rewrite

This sentence

> "The resulting transformed network remains differentiable with respect to (t) and (x), as (\Delta_0) and (\Delta_1) are treated as constants with respect to (x)."

is a little imprecise.

More precisely,

> Since (\Delta_0) and (\Delta_1) depend only on the boundary evaluations (\tilde u_\theta(t,\pm L)) and (\partial_x\tilde u_\theta(t,\pm L)), they are independent of the interior spatial coordinate (x). Therefore, when differentiating the transformed field with respect to (x), they act as coefficients multiplying the polynomial correction. However, they remain fully differentiable with respect to the network parameters and time through automatic differentiation.

That wording is mathematically cleaner.

## A stronger statement

I would even state the result as a proposition.

> **Proposition.**
> Let (\tilde u_\theta(t,x)\in C^1([-L,L])). Then the transformation defined in Eq. (\ref{eq:first_order_periodic}) produces a function (u_\theta(t,x)) satisfying
> [
> u_\theta(t,-L)=u_\theta(t,L),\qquad
> \partial_xu_\theta(t,-L)=\partial_xu_\theta(t,L)
> ]
> identically for every network parameter (\theta).

That immediately tells the reader this is an exact hard constraint rather than a penalty-based approximation.

## Generalization

What I find particularly interesting is that this construction suggests a general pattern.

* Zeroth-order periodicity → linear correction.
* First-order periodicity → cubic correction.
* Second-order periodicity → quintic correction.
* (m)-th order periodicity → polynomial of degree (2m+1).

In other words, you're effectively constructing a **Hermite interpolation polynomial** that cancels the boundary mismatches. This means ACTO can naturally be generalized to arbitrary-order periodic boundary conditions by matching derivatives up to order (m). That is a strong theoretical contribution because it provides a systematic recipe rather than isolated formulas.

Overall, I think this section is mathematically correct and quite elegant. Among the ACTO constructions you've shown so far, this is one of the cleanest because it follows directly from a constrained polynomial interpolation argument and extends naturally to higher-order periodic boundary conditions.
