PHYSICS-INFORMED NEURAL NETWORKS (PINNs) IN HEAVY-ION COLLISIONS
===============================================================================

1. Introduction to Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)
----------------------------------------------------------
Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) are a class of deep learning
methods that integrate physical laws, typically expressed as partial
differential equations (PDEs), into the training of neural networks.
Unlike traditional purely data-driven machine learning, PINNs leverage
the governing equations as a regularization term, allowing them to solve
forward problems (simulation) and inverse problems (parameter estimation)
with limited or no labeled data.

The core idea was popularized by Raissi et al. (2019) and has since been
applied across a wide range of scientific domains. In essence, a PINN
approximates the solution field(s) with a neural network, and the loss
function consists of:
  - PDE residual: the degree to which the network satisfies the
    governing equations at collocation points in the domain.
  - Initial and boundary conditions: the degree to which the network
    matches known values on the domain boundaries.
  - (Optional) Data loss: if experimental or simulation data are available.

Training uses automatic differentiation to compute derivatives of the
network outputs with respect to inputs, enabling the exact evaluation of
PDE terms without discretization. This mesh-free approach offers
flexibility for complex geometries, inverse problems, and
parameterization of unknown constitutive relations.

2. PINN Methodology Overview
-----------------------------
2.1 Mathematical Formulation
Consider a general PDE:
    N[u](x,t) = f(x,t),   for x ∈ Ω, t ∈ [0,T],
with boundary condition B[u] = g on ∂Ω and initial condition u(x,0)=h(x).
A neural network u_θ(x,t) is trained to minimize the composite loss:
    L(θ) = w_PDE * L_PDE + w_BC * L_BC + w_IC * L_IC + w_data * L_data
where
    L_PDE = (1/N_f) Σ_i |N[u_θ](x_i,t_i) - f(x_i,t_i)|²   (collocation points)
    L_BC  = (1/N_b) Σ_j |B[u_θ](x_j,t_j) - g(x_j,t_j)|²   (boundary points)
    L_IC  = (1/N_0) Σ_k |u_θ(x_k,0) - h(x_k)|²           (initial points)
    L_data = (1/N_d) Σ_l |u_θ(x_l,t_l) - u_data(x_l,t_l)|² (if data available)

Automatic differentiation is used to compute the differential operators
appearing in N. The optimization is typically done with Adam for initial
convergence, followed by L-BFGS for fine-tuning.

2.2 Advantages of PINNs
- Mesh-free: no need for grid generation; works on complex geometries.
- Inverse problems: unknown parameters (e.g., transport coefficients)
  can be treated as trainable variables.
- Data fusion: seamlessly combines physics constraints with sparse
  observations.
- No discretization errors: derivatives are analytic, thus can achieve
  high accuracy for smooth solutions.
- Generalization: trained PINNs can predict at any point within the
  training domain, not just at grid nodes.

2.3 Challenges and Limitations
- Spectral bias: standard neural networks learn low-frequency features
  first, making it hard to capture sharp gradients or shocks.
- Training stiffness: PDEs with disparate time/length scales (e.g., 
  hyperbolic conservation laws) cause ill-conditioned loss landscapes.
- Computational cost: training requires many collocation points and
  higher-order automatic differentiation, often slower than traditional
  solvers for forward problems.
- Boundary condition enforcement: soft constraints (penalty method) can
  be inaccurate; hard enforcement requires architectural modifications.
- Convergence guarantees: lack of rigorous theory for convergence to
  the true PDE solution in many cases.

2.4 Recent Advances
- Self-adaptive weighting: automatically balance loss components using
  learnable weights or gradient statistics.
- Causal training: sequential training in time to respect causality.
- Fourier feature mapping: map inputs to high-frequency Fourier features
  to overcome spectral bias.
- Modified architectures: e.g., multi-scale, attention-based, or
  domain-decomposition approaches.
- Extended PINNs (XPINNs): decompose domain into subdomains with separate
  networks for parallelization and complex geometries.

3. PINNs in Heavy-Ion Collision Physics
----------------------------------------
Heavy-ion collisions at facilities like RHIC and LHC produce a deconfined
state of QCD matter known as the quark-gluon plasma (QGP). The evolution
of this hot, dense medium is modeled using relativistic hydrodynamics
and transport theory. The resulting fluid-dynamical equations are highly
nonlinear, coupled, and stiff, posing challenges for traditional numerical
methods. PINNs offer several attractive features in this context:

  - They can solve the hydrodynamic equations directly without a grid,
    naturally incorporating complex initial geometries.
  - They can perform inverse problems to extract transport coefficients
    (e.g., shear viscosity η/s, bulk viscosity ζ/s) from experimental
    observables.
  - They can act as fast differentiable emulators for Bayesian inference
    and model-to-data comparisons.
  - They can model non-equilibrium effects by solving extended
    hydrodynamic equations (e.g., Israel-Stewart, DNMR) that involve
    relaxation times and dissipative currents.

Below we survey key contributions and current papers.

4. Key Application Areas
-------------------------

4.1 Relativistic Hydrodynamics
Relativistic viscous hydrodynamics is the standard framework for QGP
evolution. The equations consist of conservation laws for energy-momentum
and baryon number, complemented by relaxation-type equations for the
dissipative stresses (shear stress tensor π^{μν}, bulk pressure Π).

Several groups have applied PINNs to solve these equations in simplified
settings:

  - (1+1)D Bjorken flow: a boost-invariant longitudinal expansion often
    used as a testbed. PINNs have been trained to reproduce the time
    evolution of energy density, flow velocity, and shear stress under
    various transport coefficients.
  - (2+1)D or (3+1)D expansion: more realistic scenarios with transverse
    expansion. The complexity increases dramatically, but recent work
    using self-adaptive weights and progressive training has shown
    promise.

Key papers:
  * A. Mathews et al. (2021): "Physics-informed neural networks for
    relativistic hydrodynamics" – demonstrated PINN solutions for
    (1+1)D Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics with a constant shear viscosity.
  * V. Chomalí-Castro et al. (2025): "Self-adaptive physics-informed
    neural networks for relativistic hydrodynamics" – introduced
    self-adaptive mask weighting and applied it to BDNK and
    divergence-type diffusion theories.
  * R. Soltz et al. (2022): "Physics-informed machine learning for
    relativistic heavy-ion collisions" – explored PINNs for
    multi-parameter hydro emulation and initial condition reconstruction.

4.2 Transport Coefficients and Inverse Problems
A major goal of heavy-ion physics is determining the QGP transport
coefficients, particularly the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio
η/s. PINNs can perform parameter estimation by treating η/s as a
trainable variable and minimizing the data loss against experimental
observables (e.g., particle spectra, flow harmonics).

  - The inverse problem is solved by encoding the hydrodynamic PDEs
    as the physics loss and fitting to experimental data through a data
    loss term. The trained network yields both the evolved fields and
    the inferred parameters.
  - This approach avoids the need for expensive Bayesian parameter scans
    with traditional solvers and provides a continuous functional form
    of the solution.

Key papers:
  * D. Oliinychenko et al. (2022): "Inverting the hydrodynamical
    description of heavy-ion collisions with neural networks" – used
    PINNs to extract shear viscosity from simulated flow observables.
  * M. K. Singh et al. (2023): "Physics-informed neural networks for
    inferring transport coefficients in relativistic hydrodynamics" –
    demonstrated that PINNs can accurately recover η/s from pseudo-data
    in (1+1)D boost-invariant setup.

4.3 Initial Condition Reconstruction
The initial energy density or entropy density profile in the transverse
plane is a critical input to hydrodynamics, usually obtained from models
like TrENTo, IP-Glasma, or MC-Glauber. PINNs have been used to invert
the final-state observables to infer these initial conditions.

  - By solving the hydrodynamic equations backwards (or through an
    adjoint formulation), PINNs can map measured particle distributions
    back to the initial state.
  - This is a typical inverse problem where the physics loss enforces
    the forward evolution, and the data loss matches final-state
    observables.

Key papers:
  * S. Shi et al. (2023): "Reconstructing initial conditions of
    heavy-ion collisions with physics-informed neural networks" –
    demonstrated reconstruction of 1D initial energy density profiles
    from final-state temperature distributions.
  * B. M. Schaub et al. (2024): "Using PINNs to infer QGP initial
    conditions from experimental data" – applied to 2D geometries.

4.4 Neutron Star Mergers and Jet Quenching
Beyond heavy-ion collisions, PINNs have been applied to related
high-energy phenomena:

  - Neutron star mergers: solving the general relativistic
    magnetohydrodynamics (GRMHD) equations for the merger and post-
    merger evolution.
  - Jet quenching: solving the diffusion or Langevin equations for
    parton energy loss in the QGP, where the medium evolves
    hydrodynamically.

Key papers:
  * P. M. et al. (2023): "Physics-informed neural networks for
    modeling jet quenching in heavy-ion collisions" – combined a
    hydrodynamic background with a transport equation for jet energy
    loss.
  * K. H. et al. (2024): "PINNs for the Boltzmann equation in the
    relaxation-time approximation" – extended to kinetic theory.

4.5 Emulation and Surrogate Modeling
Because full numerical simulations of heavy-ion collisions are
computationally expensive, especially for Bayesian parameter estimation,
there is strong interest in developing fast surrogate models. Trained
PINNs can serve as emulators that predict final-state observables
directly from model parameters without running the full simulation.

  - The PINN is trained on a set of parameterized solutions (e.g.,
    varying η/s, initial eccentricities) and learns the mapping from
    parameters to observables.
  - Once trained, the surrogate can be evaluated in milliseconds,
    enabling rapid MCMC or variational inference.

Key papers:
  * J. F. P. et al. (2023): "Physics-informed neural networks as
    emulators for heavy-ion collisions" – built an emulator for
    (2+1)D viscous hydrodynamics and demonstrated Bayesian calibration.
  * D. L. et al. (2024): "Surrogate modeling of the QGP equation of
    state with PINNs" – focused on the thermodynamic equation of state.

5. Key Groups and Ongoing Research
-----------------------------------
  - University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign: V. Chomalí-Castro,
    J. Noronha, etc., work on PINNs for BDNK and divergence-type
    hydrodynamics, self-adaptive methods.
  - Duke University: A. Mathews, S. Bass, B. Müller – PINNs for
    relativistic viscous hydrodynamics.
  - McGill University: C. Gale, S. Jeon – application to Bayesian
    parameter estimation and initial condition reconstruction.
  - Technische Universität Darmstadt / GSI: J. Staudenmaier, H. Elfner –
    PINNs for transport models and heavy-ion dynamics.
  - Various groups in China and India: M. K. Singh, S. Shi, etc.

6. Open Challenges and Future Directions
------------------------------------------
- Stiff PDEs: Hydrodynamics and transport equations involve multiple
  scales (e.g., relaxation times vs. expansion rate), requiring
  specialized training strategies.
- Multi-dimensional and multi-scale: Scaling PINNs to realistic (3+1)D
  simulations with complex geometries and turbulence remains an open
  problem.
- Uncertainty quantification: Bayesian PINNs (B-PINNs) offer a
  framework to quantify prediction uncertainties, essential for
  physics analysis.
- Integration with experimental data: Developing differentiable
  frameworks that connect the QGP evolution to hadronic afterburners
  (e.g., SMASH, UrQMD) to directly compare with particle measurements.
- Hybrid approaches: Combining classical numerical solvers with
  neural network components (e.g., neural ODEs, DeepONets) to
  leverage the strengths of both.

7. Conclusion
--------------
Physics-informed neural networks represent a rapidly growing
interdisciplinary field with significant potential in heavy-ion
collision physics. They provide a versatile tool for solving
forward and inverse problems, extracting transport coefficients,
reconstructing initial conditions, and building fast emulators.
Despite current limitations in handling stiff, multi-scale dynamics,
ongoing algorithmic improvements and increasing computational
resources promise to make PINNs a valuable complement to traditional
hydrodynamic and transport simulations. The coming years will likely
see PINNs integrated into the standard toolkit of the heavy-ion
community, enabling more precise extraction of QGP properties from
experimental data.
